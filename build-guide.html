Build Guide

Build a botanical sounding rocket

From an empty workbench to a flight-ready rocket carrying a living payload — engineered so the biology survives the ride and the data is clean enough for NASA OSDR.

⚠️ Safety & legality first. High-power rocketry involves energetic materials. Build and fly under the rules of a recognized body such as the National Association of Rocketry (NAR) or Tripoli, follow your national and local regulations (in the U.S., FAA notification/waivers may be required), and always launch at a sanctioned range with certified mentors. This guide is educational and is not a substitute for formal certification and on-site supervision. edit for your org

What you're building

A recoverable single-stage rocket with a dedicated biology payload bay. The novelty isn't the rocket — it's that the payload is alive, and every subsystem exists to keep it that way and to record what it experienced.

🧩

Airframe

Body tubes, nose cone, fins, and motor mount — the structure that gets your payload up and back down intact.

🌱

Payload bay

A sealed, instrumented capsule holding seeds or seedlings plus a sensor stack to log what they endured.

🪂

Recovery

Parachute / dual-deploy so the living payload lands soft enough to study afterward.

The build, step by step

Work top to bottom. Don't skip the experiment-design step — a flight with no control group produces a flight, not science.

Design the experiment first

Before any glue, decide what biological question this flight answers. This determines your payload, your sensors, and what OSDR will eventually accept.

  • Hypothesis: e.g. "High-G launch + brief altitude exposure reduces germination rate in Arabidopsis vs. ground control." your hypothesis
  • Organism: choose a fast, well-characterized species (Arabidopsis, radish, lettuce) so results are comparable.
  • Controls: an identical ground-control payload that never flies is mandatory.
  • Variables you can log: peak acceleration (G), max altitude, temperature, flight duration.
Tip: read the OSDR Training page now — designing to the standard is far easier than retrofitting your data later.

Gather materials & tools

Source your kit before building. Quantities below are a starting checklist — adjust to your design.

Sizing matters: the live payload + sensors add mass. Run your numbers in a simulator (OpenRocket / RockSim) so the motor and recovery are sized for the loaded rocket, not an empty one.

Simulate & finalize the design

Model the loaded rocket in OpenRocket (free) before cutting anything.

  • Confirm stability margin of ~1–2 calibers with the payload installed.
  • Check predicted apogee, max velocity, and peak acceleration — that G figure is a key biological variable, so know it before you fly.
  • Verify recovery descent rate is gentle enough that the payload survives landing.

Build the airframe

Assemble the structure. Take your time on alignment — a crooked fin is a failed flight.

  • Mount the motor tube with centering rings; cure fully.
  • Attach fins square and symmetric (a fin-alignment jig helps); fillet with epoxy.
  • Install the recovery harness anchor and prep the nose-cone/coupler joints.

Build the botanical payload bay

This is the heart of a MadWest rocket. The capsule must protect the organism and record its environment.

  • Containment: secure seeds in a medium (agar plate, moist substrate, or sealed seed-pack) that won't shift under launch loads.
  • Cushioning: foam or a sprung mount to dampen vibration and landing shock.
  • Sensors: log acceleration, temperature, and pressure for the whole flight — these become your environmental metadata.
  • Sealing & sterility: keep the bay clean so post-flight contamination doesn't confound your biology results.
Keep a twin. Build the ground-control payload identically and prepare it on the same timeline — same seeds, same medium, same handling, minus the flight.

Integrate electronics & recovery

Wire and test the avionics on the bench before they ever go in the rocket.

  • Confirm the altimeter arms, logs, and triggers deployment in a ground test.
  • Pack the parachute; verify the deployment charge or mechanism (dual-deploy if your altitude needs it).
  • Label and time-sync every data logger so flight data and biology samples can be matched later.

Pre-flight checks & launch

At a sanctioned range, under mentor supervision, run the checklist.

  • Final mass & stability check with the live payload installed.
  • Load seeds/seedlings as late as possible; record the exact load time.
  • Arm avionics, get range-safety sign-off, and launch.
  • Note launch time, conditions, and motor used — all of it is metadata.

Recover, measure & preserve

The flight is the easy part — the science happens now.

  • Recover quickly; record landing time and any damage.
  • Download flight logs (altitude, G, temperature) immediately.
  • Begin your biology assay on flown and control samples together: germination counts, growth measurements over days, optional gene-expression sampling.
  • Photograph and log everything with consistent units and timestamps.

Package & submit to OSDR

Turn the raw results into an openly shareable dataset.

  • Organize flight metadata + biology results to the OSDR structure.
  • Follow the OSDR Training checklist, then the Data Submission workflow.
  • Document your methods so another club could repeat your flight.
That final submission is what makes this more than a hobby launch — your flight becomes reusable science.
