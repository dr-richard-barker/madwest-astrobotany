TITLE
Botanical sounding rockets as an accessible space-biology platform: flowering
and stress-gene responses in flown Arabidopsis, and an ectomycorrhiza x Populus
tremula survival experiment from a high-school and AstroBotany partnership

ABSTRACT (text only; 300-word limit)
Spaceflight imposes a distinctive stress on plants: orbital transcriptomics
(BRIC-19 / TOAST) reveal a high-light-like, reactive oxygen species signature
involving heat-shock proteins (HSP101, HSP70) and WRKY33, even in darkness (Choi
et al., 2019). Such experiments are powerful but costly and rare. We asked
whether accessible, repeatable suborbital sounding rockets can let students probe
flight-associated plant biology and contribute open data. A high-school team,
mentored by the University of Wisconsin–Madison AstroBotany laboratory, ran three
2018 launches (Cesaroni K1620 motor). Launch 1 (Arabidopsis test): flown
seedlings reportedly flowered earlier, and co-flown ectomycorrhizal spores stayed
viable after flight. Launch 2 (Arabidopsis, repeat): qPCR of stress genes (MPK3,
TCH3, CBP60g; UBQ10 reference) showed flown plants trending toward lower stress
and touch signaling (MPK3 0.34x, TCH3 0.56x) but higher defense signaling
(CBP60g 1.60x) versus lab controls (n=4; trends only). Launch 3 ("TREES," a
codename): Populus tremula seedlings with or without ectomycorrhizal (ECM)
inoculation, flown versus ground, imaged as a timelapse to test whether the
symbiosis aids flight survival. Automated green-canopy analysis of about 2,600
frames showed the opposite of the hypothesis: mycorrhizal seedlings peaked, then
died back to 71-73% of peak canopy, whereas non-mycorrhizal plants held near peak
(94-96%); the decline is visible in the footage. Imaging windows differed by
treatment, so this is a strong trend rather than controlled proof. All results
are classroom-scale and hypothesis-generating, but demonstrate a complete
build-fly-measure-deposit pipeline culminating in ISA-formatted data prepared for
NASA's Open Science Data Repository. Botanical sounding rockets are a tractable
entry point for student-led space biology.
